# Matsubayashi-ryū technical vocabulary — a reference

Karate vocabulary is mostly Japanese kanji compounds, and knowing the characters often reveals structure that pronunciation alone obscures. *Zenkutsu-dachi* is 前屈立ち — front-bent stance. *Gyaku-zuki* is 逆突き — reverse punch. Once you see the characters, the logic of the naming falls out almost by itself.

This is a working reference — the technical vocabulary I find myself reaching for in Matsubayashi-ryū practice, organized by category, with kanji where I'm confident of the standard form. I expect to revise it as I learn. Italicized entries carry brief notes on distinctive usage or Matsubayashi-specific emphasis. Terms marked with an Okinawan origin (rather than a Japanese kanji reading) appear without kanji where there isn't a standard character form.

This reference follows the organization in Nagamine's *Essence of Okinawan Karate-Dō* where applicable, extended with terms that appear in regular dōjō practice but not always in the published literature.

## Stances (立ち / tachi, dachi)

| Term | Kanji | Meaning |
|------|-------|---------|
| shizen-tai | 自然体 | natural body; basic ready posture |
| shizen-dachi | 自然立ち | natural stance |
| heisoku-dachi | 閉足立ち | feet-together stance |
| musubi-dachi | 結び立ち | heels together, toes out 45° |
| hachiji-dachi | 八字立ち | "figure-eight" stance; feet at shoulder width, toes slightly out |
| heikō-dachi | 平行立ち | parallel stance |
| uchi-hachiji-dachi | 内八字立ち | inward figure-eight; feet slightly pigeon-toed |
| zenkutsu-dachi | 前屈立ち | front-bent stance; weight forward |
| kōkutsu-dachi | 後屈立ち | back-bent stance; weight on rear leg |
| naihanchi-dachi | 内歩進立ち | Naihanchi stance; feet turned slightly inward, as if riding |
| shiko-dachi | 四股立ち | sumo-style stance; feet turned out 45°, deep squat |
| jigotai-dachi | 自護体立ち | "self-protecting body" stance; in some dōjō usage equivalent to shiko-dachi |
| neko-ashi-dachi | 猫足立ち | cat stance; weight on rear leg, front foot light on ball |
| sagi-ashi-dachi | 鷺足立ち | heron stance; one leg raised, other supporting |
| kōsa-dachi | 交差立ち | crossed-leg stance |
| sanchin-dachi | 三戦立ち | "three-battles" stance; hourglass, tension throughout |
| naname-zenkutsu-dachi | 斜め前屈立ち | diagonal front-bent stance |

Matsubayashi favors the naturally-depthed stances (shizen-tai, zenkutsu, neko-ashi) over the very deep stances (shiko, sanchin). This is a deliberate design choice about mobility which I discuss separately in the article on [the three aspects of a stance](32-the-three-aspects-of-a-stance).

## Okinawan body concepts

Three terms stand apart from the broader Japanese karate vocabulary because they come from Okinawan rather than mainland Japanese usage. Matsubayashi inherits them along with the rest of its Okinawan technical language.

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| gamaku | core / hip connection; the coordinated use of the pelvic girdle to generate power through the body |
| chinkuchi | connective tension at the moment of kime; the instantaneous whole-body bracing that transmits force |
| muchimi | "heavy stickiness"; a quality of weighted, adhesive motion — especially in Tomari-te and Naha-te derived movements |

These three concepts describe how Okinawan karate generates and transmits power in a way that distinguishes it from harder Japanese styles. They are hard to teach verbally and easier to recognize in the room with a teacher who has them.

## A note on transliteration

There are several romanization systems for Japanese. This article uses a modified Hepburn system: long vowels with macrons (ō, ū), distinct *shi/chi/tsu* for the relevant moras, *n* before labials written as *n* rather than *m*. This diverges slightly from some older karate books, which may write *dojo* for *dōjō*, *shuto* for *shutō*, *Goju* for *Gōjū*, and so on. The spellings here aim to preserve the vowel lengths that matter for pronunciation.

Some Okinawan-origin terms (*gamaku*, *chinkuchi*, *muchimi*) do not have widely standardized kanji representations and appear here without them.
