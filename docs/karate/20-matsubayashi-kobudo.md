# Matsubayashi kobudō — a distinct tradition

In Matsubayashi-ryū, the empty-hand tradition and the weapons tradition are not the same lineage wearing two faces. They share a common root — Shōshin Nagamine, who carried both — but the transmissions are separate, the historical figures are different, and the technical vocabulary of each draws on different streams of Okinawan martial culture. Understanding this is part of understanding what Matsubayashi kobudō actually is.

## Two lineages from one root

The empty-hand lineage in Matsubayashi runs through Nagamine's three principal teachers: Ankichi Arakaki, Chōtoku Kyan, and Chōki Motobu. Its technical debt is to Shuri-te and Tomari-te.

The weapons lineage runs through a different network. The pivotal figure for its Bay Area transmission is **Chōtoku Omine** (1929–1975), one of Nagamine's senior students, who brought Matsubayashi-ryū — both empty hand and weapons — to California in 1969. His early death in 1975 cut the transmission short, but what he carried had already taken root.

The deeper historical source is **Shinei Kyan** (1912–1997) — a peer of Nagamine, not to be confused with Chōtoku Kyan, who was Nagamine's empty-hand teacher. Shinei Kyan drew on two major streams: **Kina Shōsei** for saijutsu (in the Ufuchiku tradition) and **Ōshiro Chōjō** (Izumikawa line) for bōjutsu (Yamanni-ryū). These two sources placed the Matsubayashi weapons curriculum at the intersection of the principal surviving Okinawan kobudō lineages. The bō and sai kata names carry their memory forward: Kyan no Sai takes its name from Shinei Kyan directly.

## The weapons and kata

Matsubayashi-ryū kobudō trains five principal weapons, with the eku (boat oar) carried in some lineages as a sixth:

| Weapon | Kata |
|--------|------|
| Bō (棒, 6-foot staff) | Shiromatsu no Kon, Shiratarō no Kon, Sakugawa no Kon, Shūshi no Kon, Chōun no Kon |
| Sai (釵) | Sai Dai Ichi (Chomō Hanagusuku no Sai), Sai Dai Ni (Kyan no Sai) |
| Nunchaku (ヌンチャク) | Nunchaku Shō, Maezato no Shō |
| Tonfa (トンファー) | Tonfa no Kata |
| Kama (鎌, sickle) | Kishaba no Kama |
| Eku (エーク, oar) | Tsuken Sunakake no Kon Eku |

The bō curriculum is the largest and oldest. **Shiratarō no Kon** (白樽の棍) comes directly through the Yamanni-ryū line and can be seen demonstrated by Shinei Kyan himself on film — a rare direct record of the tradition's weapons source. **Shiromatsu no Kon** and **Shūshi no Kon** are the other foundational pieces of the bō curriculum, both carrying material from the same stream.

The kama kata, **Kishaba no Kama**, takes its name from Chōkei Kishaba, an early member of the Honbu dōjō whose contribution to the weapons curriculum is preserved in this single piece. It is among the more demanding kata in the system to execute well.

The sai kata carry their own lineage markers. Sai Dai Ichi, **Chomō Hanagusuku no Sai**, is named for the old Okinawan master Hanagusuku Chomō; Sai Dai Ni, **Kyan no Sai**, for Shinei Kyan. Both can be seen demonstrated by Takayoshi Nagamine, the 2nd Sōke, in footage that serves as the primary reference for the lineage.

The **eku** (wooden boat oar) is the most distinctly Okinawan of the weapons — the improvised weapon of a coastal people, adapted from the tool used to propel fishing boats. **Tsuken Sunakake no Kon Eku** comes from Tsuken island and carries the technique of throwing sand (*sunakake*) as a blinding feint before the weapon strike.

## A tradition enriched by several sources

Like the empty-hand curriculum, the weapons tradition in my branch has been deepened by additional streams of teaching over the decades.

**Hanshi Toshimitsu Arakaki**, Vice-President of the World Matsubayashi-ryū Karate-do Association, has contributed to both Shūshi no Kon and the bunkai of Shiromatsu no Kon. Arakaki Sensei trained under Hōhan Sōken before Nagamine, and his weapons understanding reflects the depth of that additional lineage.

**Ōshiro Toshihiro**, one of the leading figures of Yamanni-ryū bōjutsu, has taught seminars enriching the bō principles underlying the curriculum. Yamanni-ryū is one of the two main streams from which the Matsubayashi weapons tradition descends, so this enrichment goes directly to the roots.

**Steve Gombosi** carried the Kishaba no Kama transmission into our branch — the kama kata from the Honbu dōjō.

The most recent addition came through **Sensei Jordan Du Val's 2023 trip to Okinawa**, where he trained with Miyagi Sensei on **Tsuken Sunakake no Kon Eku**. The eku extends the curriculum into a weapon type not covered by the five-weapon core, and its arrival in our dōjō is a direct consequence of that trip.

## Why keep the lineages distinct in the mind

The practical reason to understand the two lineages as distinct is accuracy. When you perform a bō kata in Matsubayashi-ryū, you are not performing something Nagamine derived from his empty-hand principles in the 1940s. You are performing something that arrived at Nagamine through Shinei Kyan and the Yamanni-ryū and Ufuchiku streams — with their own technical logic, their own historical figures, their own relationship to Okinawan material culture.

The empty-hand kata have Shuri and Tomari behind them. The weapons kata have the bō masters and sai masters of coastal Okinawa behind them. Both are Matsubayashi. Neither is reducible to the other.
