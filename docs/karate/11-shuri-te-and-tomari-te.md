# Shuri-te and Tomari-te in Matsubayashi

Before there was Matsubayashi-ryū, before there was Shōrin-ryū, before any of the styles that Western practitioners recognize today, the old masters of Okinawa trained in three places: Shuri, Tomari, and Naha. Each town had its own way of doing *tī* (手, "hand" — the old Okinawan word for the unarmed martial tradition). These became the three streams known as **Shuri-te**, **Tomari-te**, and **Naha-te**.

The streams were not schools in the modern sense. They were dense local traditions, passed between kin and close students, each shaped by the specific physical culture and social role of its town. When these streams were formalized into named styles in the early 20th century, most lineages descended primarily from one stream: Gōjū-ryū from Naha-te, Shōtōkan from Shuri-te, and so on.

Matsubayashi-ryū is unusual in preserving **both** the Shuri-te and Tomari-te streams. Nagamine made this a deliberate choice, reflecting the three teachers from whom he learned: Ankichi Arakaki, Chōtoku Kyan, and Chōki Motobu. All three carried material from both Shuri and Tomari, and Nagamine chose to preserve rather than simplify.

## Shuri-te

Shuri was the royal capital of the Ryūkyū kingdom. The Shuri-te tradition developed among the *shizoku* class — the aristocratic warrior-officials who served the king, and among whom fell the duty of personal protection of the royal person and the defense of the court. The defining figure of the tradition is **Matsumura Sōkon** (1809–1898), known as *Bushi* Matsumura, the king's bodyguard and the synthesizer of the Shuri-te lineage as Matsubayashi inherits it.

Shuri-te's characteristics, as they come down through Matsumura to Itosu to Kyan and into Matsubayashi, include:

- **Upright, mobile stances.** The Shuri-te practitioner was often working in confined spaces — courtyards, corridors, the close work of bodyguarding — and developed a stance vocabulary that favored agility over depth. Matsubayashi's shallow zenkutsu-dachi and the lightness of its neko-ashi-dachi reflect this heritage.
- **Linear techniques with strong projection.** Many Shuri-te strikes extend directly from the hips and shoulders, with the emphasis on fast, clean commitment rather than tension-heavy delivery.
- **Long-range engagement.** Shuri-te tends to prefer keeping distance and striking from range, rather than grappling or close-quarters infighting.

The Shuri-te kata in Matsubayashi's 18 are: the five Pinans (Itosu's pedagogical distillations), the three Naihanchi (ancient at the Shodan level, Itosu-constructed at Nidan and Sandan), Gōjūshiho (from the Matsumura line), and Kūsankū (the 1761 envoy's teaching, preserved through Tōde Sakugawa to Matsumura). Ananku sits ambiguously — it is Kyan's own creation and draws on multiple sources, but is generally classified with the Shuri-te material.

That's eight (or nine, counting Ananku) of the 18 kata.

## Tomari-te

Tomari, a coastal village north of Shuri, was a different world. Where Shuri-te grew among aristocrats, Tomari-te grew among merchants, fishermen, and smaller farmers — and among the sailors and foreign visitors who passed through Tomari's port. The tradition preserved older kata and incorporated techniques brought by shipwrecked travelers, most famously the Chinese sailor Chintō who is said to have taught the master Matsumora Kōsaku in the mid-19th century.

The defining figure of Tomari-te is **Matsumora Kōsaku** (1829–1898), a contemporary of Matsumura Sōkon but a very different kind of teacher. Matsumora was not a court official. He was a village-level martial master who preserved and refined a distinct body of material.

Tomari-te's characteristics, as they reach Matsubayashi:

- **Lower, more grounded stances.** Tomari-te practitioners were often working in more open terrain and in less socially-restricted contexts than Shuri-te. Their stance work tends to sit deeper and to emphasize rooting.
- **Close-range and trapping techniques.** Tomari-te preserved techniques for closer combat, including hand-trapping, redirection, and short-range striking — less polished than Shuri-te's longer lines, but more immediate in application.
- **A rougher, more practical sensibility.** Tomari-te is sometimes described as less refined than Shuri-te, meaning not worse but less beholden to ceremonial elegance. The emphasis was on function.

The Tomari-te kata in Matsubayashi's 18 are: **Wankan**, **Rōhai**, **Wanshū**, **Chintō**, and **Passai** (via Oyadomari, not via Matsumura — this is an important distinction).

That is five of the 18 kata.

## The Oyadomari Passai — a specific inheritance

Passai is a kata that exists in multiple lineages. The famous Shōtōkan version traces through Matsumura Sōkon; it is the Shuri-te Passai, and is better known in the West. But Matsubayashi's Passai comes through a different line: **Oyadomari Kōkan** of Tomari, a contemporary of Matsumora and a preserver of the Tomari-te Passai.

Kyan Chōtoku, Nagamine's principal teacher, learned both Passai versions but transmitted the Oyadomari Tomari-te version into his own teaching. This is the version Matsubayashi preserves. It differs from the Matsumura Passai in its opening, its rhythm, and several key sequences. A Matsubayashi practitioner who compares their Passai with a Shōtōkan practitioner's will quickly notice that the kata are relatives, not twins.

This is a specific inheritance Nagamine made through Kyan, and it is worth knowing if you ever train with practitioners of other Shōrin branches. The difference is not an error; it is the mark of a deliberate lineage choice.

## Why keep both?

Nagamine's preservation of both streams is a statement. The two traditions illuminate each other. Shuri-te's elegance and Tomari-te's rougher practicality check each other; neither becomes an ideology. The Matsubayashi practitioner who learns Pinan Shodan (Shuri) and then Wankan (Tomari) experiences two different bodies of answers to the same basic problem — how to use one's body to end a violent encounter — and the contrast is itself educational.

It also keeps a broader inheritance alive. Some Shōrin lineages have essentially lost the Tomari-te material, preserving only the Shuri-te line. Matsubayashi's insistence on keeping both has helped to keep Tomari-te alive as a distinct living tradition within Okinawan karate.

## And Naha-te?

Naha-te is the third stream. Matsubayashi does not principally descend from it. Naha-te's major living descendant is Gōjū-ryū, founded by Miyagi Chōjun from the teachings of Higaonna Kanryō. Gōjū-ryū and Matsubayashi are related through Okinawan karate's shared origins but are distinct traditions with distinct physical vocabularies — Naha-te's emphasis on sanchin-based tension and breath, its close-range grappling and trapping — that Matsubayashi does not primarily carry.

There is one fingerprint of Naha-te in Matsubayashi, though: **Fukyūgata Ni** was created by Miyagi Chōjun, the founder of Gōjū-ryū, in the early 1940s. When Nagamine adopted Fukyūgata Ni as one of the foundation kata of Matsubayashi, he brought a piece of Naha-te into the curriculum. A small piece — Fukyūgata Ni is deliberately pedagogical, not a deep Naha-te kata in the sense that Sanchin is — but it is a real acknowledgment.

So the three historical streams are all present in Matsubayashi, in proportion: Shuri-te most centrally, Tomari-te prominently, Naha-te in a single introductory kata that honors its founder's contemporary. This is, I think, part of what Nagamine meant when he called his system a synthesis.

- Shōshin Nagamine, *Tales of Okinawa's Great Masters* — biographical sketches of Matsumura, Matsumora, Kyan, Motobu, and others, translated by Patrick McCarthy.
