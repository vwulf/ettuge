## 1. Theoretical Foundation: The Hybrid Origin of BrÄhmÄ«

The "Hybrid Origin" theory, as advanced by Karan Damodaram Pillai (2023), posits that the BrÄhmÄ« scriptâ€”the foundational ancestor of the modern Kannada scriptâ€”was not a singular indigenous invention nor a linear descendant of a solitary foreign script. Rather, it represents a sophisticated, systematic hybrid created by Indian scholars. This analysis argues that these ancient linguists synthesized a new script by selecting and adapting specific characters from Aramaic, Phoenician, and Greek sources, likely facilitated by Western Semitic maritime traders.

The historical impetus for this orthographic adaptation was the fall of the Persian Empire in 330 BC. Indian scholars, requiring a more expansive phonemic register to accommodate the 43 phonemes of Prakrit and Sanskrit (which the 22-letter Semitic abjads could not cover), engaged in a deliberate intellectual synthesis. This resulted in a "hybrid invention" that retained the phonetic rigor of the Indic lapidary tradition while utilizing the graphical skeletons of Mediterranean and Near Eastern characters.

## 4. Summary of Indic Structural Innovations

The transition from Semitic and Greek prototypes to BrÄhmÄ« involved several systematic modifications designed to align foreign forms with Indic linguistic requirements:

- **Loops for Aspiration:** The addition of a loop was the standardized orthographic marker for aspiration (e.g., pa ğ‘€§ to pha ğ‘€¨; ca ğ‘€˜ to cha ğ‘€™).
- **Horizontal Extensions:** Midline or top horizontal strokes were used to denote phonetic strengthening, such as vowel length (the midline stroke in Ä) or retroflexion (the top horizontal line in á¹‡a ğ‘€¡).
- **Rotation and Reversal:** To accommodate the shift from the right-to-left direction of Semitic scripts to the left-to-right direction of Indic scripts, many characters were rotated or mirrored (e.g., á¸a, la, ha).
- **Phonemic Splitting:** Scholars split single Semitic prototypes into multiple Indic characters to satisfy Sanskrit requirements. Most notably, the Semitic á¹£ade (ğ¤‘) was adapted into three distinct characters: ca (ğ‘€˜), sa (ğ‘€²), and á¹£a (ğ‘€±).
- **Geometric Adaptation:** Characters were often derived through geometric logic, such as the "halving of the circle" to differentiate the non-aspirate á¹­a from the aspirate á¹­ha.
