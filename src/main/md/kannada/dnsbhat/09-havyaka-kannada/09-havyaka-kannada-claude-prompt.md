## BOOK OVERVIEW

*Havyaka Kannaḍa* is DNS Bhat's popular Kannada-language account of the Havyaka dialect — the variety of Kannada spoken by the Havyaka Brahmin community of coastal Karnataka (centred around Puttur, Uppinangadi, and surrounding areas of South Kanara district). DNS Bhat himself was born into this community, giving the book both scholarly depth and personal intimacy.

The book compares Havyaka systematically with: (1) standard written Kannada; (2) Old Kannada inscriptional texts; (3) other coastal Karnataka dialects (Halaki Gowda Kannada, other Gowda dialects); and (4) other regional Havyaka varieties (Kumta, Sirsi, Siddapura, Sagar areas). Its central scholarly argument is that **Havyaka Kannada preserves certain Proto-Dravidian phonological and morphological features that are not found in written Kannada records** — making it an irreplaceable source for the reconstruction of Kannada's linguistic history. Its "archaic" features are not due to cultural conservatism or stagnation but to independent dialect evolution.

This is a popular work — accessible to literate Kannada readers without linguistics training. The companion academic work is *An Outline Grammar of Havyaka* (1971, Deccan College, Poona), which provides the full technical phonological and morphological description with paradigm tables.

**Note on source quality:** ~82% of transcript slots are cleaned. Parts 1–14 (early sections) are the most affected by garbling due to poor source audio. Best content in Parts 11, 27–29. The English summary (`-en.md`) and Eke romanisation (`-kn-eke.md`) draw on the best available parts.

## CORE ARGUMENT (CENTRAL THESIS)

1. **Havyaka is a fully independent Kannada dialect** — not a "corrupted" or "simplified" form of standard Kannada, but a systematic variety with its own phonological rules, morphological paradigms, and lexical patterns.

2. **Havyaka preserves Proto-Dravidian features lost in written Kannada** — certain phonological and morphological patterns in Havyaka match reconstructed Proto-Dravidian forms better than standard written Kannada does. Written Kannada has undergone changes (Sanskrit influence, spelling standardisation, societal prestige norms) that have moved it away from these ancestral forms.

3. **Dialect variation reveals the true depth of Kannada's Dravidian roots** — by comparing Havyaka with Old Kannada inscriptions, DNS Bhat shows that features of Havyaka that look "archaic" or "non-standard" are in fact older Dravidian forms preserved in speech but lost in writing.

4. **The Havyaka community spans a wide geographic area** — Puttur, Uppinangadi, and inland areas of South Kanara; inland to Kumta, Sirsi, Siddapura, Sagar in Uttara Kannada. Different areas show different phonological and lexical features; DNS Bhat documents all of them.

5. **Systematic phonological correspondences link Havyaka to standard Kannada and other Dravidian languages** — the same comparative method used to prove Dravidian genetic relationships applies within Kannada dialectology.

### Preamble — Introduction (Parts P1–P6)
- DNS Bhat's personal connection to the Havyaka community
- Purpose and scope of the study
- Relationship between this popular work and the 1971 academic grammar

### Theme 1 — The Havyaka Community and Dialect Geography (Parts 1–10)
- Havyaka Brahmins: community identity, geographic distribution in South and Uttara Kannada
- Sub-varieties by region: Puttur (core), Uppinangadi, Kumta, Sirsi, Siddapura, Sagar — and systematic differences among them
- Relationship to neighbouring coastal communities: Halaki Gowda, Gowda Kannada, Tulu-speaking communities

### Theme 2 — Historical and Comparative Background (Parts 11–14)
- Historical documentation of Havyaka in Old Kannada inscriptions
- How Havyaka diverged from Old Kannada — timeline and key changes
- Relationship to other Dravidian languages (Tamil, Tulu, Malayalam, Kodagu) via systematic correspondences

### Theme 3 — Phonological Correspondences Across Dialects (Parts 15–22)
- Systematic vowel correspondences: Havyaka often preserves older vowel forms
- Consonant correspondences: retroflex, dental, velar patterns
- Initial consonant clusters in Havyaka vs. standard Kannada (e.g., *p-* → *h-* changes)
- Geminate consonants and their fate in different varieties
- These correspondences mirror the patterns used in inter-language Dravidian comparative work

### Theme 4 — Morphological Paradigms: Verb Forms (Parts 23–29)
- Havyaka verb morphology: past tense markers, negative forms, non-finite converbs
- How Havyaka verb endings compare to standard Kannada paradigms and to Old Kannada
- Proto-Dravidian reconstruction: which Havyaka forms are plausibly archaic
- Noun case: postposition patterns; how Havyaka handles dative, locative, instrumental
- Personal pronoun system in Havyaka vs. standard Kannada

### Theme 5 — Word Classification and Grammar (Parts 30–37)
- Havyaka vocabulary: unique lexical items not found in standard Kannada (or found in Old Kannada only)
- Word formation patterns: which Dravidian suffixes are productive in Havyaka
- Compound verbs and their patterns in Havyaka
- Sanskrit loanword integration: how Havyaka modifies Sanskrit loans phonologically
