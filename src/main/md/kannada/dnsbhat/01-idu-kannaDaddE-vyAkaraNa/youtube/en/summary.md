## Overview

*Idu KannaDaddE VyAkaraNa* ("This is Kannada's Own Grammar") is DNS Bhat's comprehensive grammar of written Kannada, published as a single consolidated volume from his earlier 7-volume *Kannada Barahada Sollarime* series. The book's central argument is that existing Kannada grammars are not genuinely Kannada grammars at all — they merely transplant the rules of Sanskrit grammar (and to a lesser extent English grammar) onto Kannada, which has an entirely different structure. Bhat spent from 2010 to 2019 writing down what a genuinely Kannada grammar looks like, and this 2021 book collects that work. The grammar is described as primarily a grammar of *written* Kannada — because written Kannada is more uniform across regions than spoken Kannada, and a grammar of written Kannada can actually serve as a practical standard for all Kannada writers. Alongside the grammatical description, Bhat advocates two concrete reforms: reducing unnecessary Sanskrit loanwords in Kannada prose, and respelling the Sanskrit borrowings that remain as Kannadigas actually pronounce them — so that the written language becomes accessible to everyone, not just the educated upper class.

> *Preface (Part 1):* "The Kannada grammars in use today are not genuinely Kannada grammars — instead of explaining the rules by which Kannada words, word-groups, and sentences are formed, they attempt to apply the rules of Sanskrit or English grammar to Kannada. I showed this about twenty years ago in my book *Kannadakke Beku KannaDaddE VyAkaraNa*. From 2010 to 2019 I undertook the work of showing what a genuinely Kannada grammar looks like, and published those findings in seven separate volumes under the name *Kannada Barahada Sollarime*. I am now collecting all those pieces together and publishing them as this book, *Idu KannaDaddE VyAkaraNa*."

## Part 1 — Preface: Why Kannada Needs Its Own Grammar

[Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wBaRf2lMGic)
[Transcript →](../kn/full#part-1)

> **Coverage note:** Part 1 is the preface and introduction. Transcript is uncleaned auto-captions but fully readable. Contains the book's complete table of contents (ಒಳಪಿಡಿ) in the transcript, giving chapter-level visibility into the full 19-chapter structure.

- **The core complaint:** Existing Kannada school grammars are not Kannada grammars — they use Sanskrit grammar (Pāṇinian *vyākaraṇa*) as their model. Early Kannada scholars could only *apply* Sanskrit rules to Kannada because the weight of Pāṇini's framework blocked them from discovering Kannada's own rules. Those who came after could not escape that trap either. Combined with the language changes from Old Kannada to Modern Kannada, no one was able to update the grammar correctly. The result: even today, school grammar rules cannot be illustrated using Modern Kannada words and sentences alone
- **Grammar is not foreign:** Bhat insists grammar is not some strange, difficult puzzle. It simply describes the rules everyone already uses unconsciously when they speak or write. We all use hundreds of grammatical rules fluently — we just cannot articulate them. Grammar makes the unconscious conscious
  - Example: every Kannada speaker correctly chooses among the three forms of the dative suffix — *ge*, *ige*, *ke* — without ever being taught a rule. Grammar explains *what* that rule is
  - Example: the *conjunctive participle* (*-u/-i* joining form) can only join two clauses when both have the same agent. *Rāju manege bandu ūTa māDida* ("Raju came home and ate") is correct; *Rāju manege bandu Jānaki ūTa māDiDaLu* is wrong — the two agents differ. Kannada speakers know this intuitively; grammar states it explicitly
- **This grammar is a written grammar:** The grammar describes written Kannada, not any particular spoken dialect. Written Kannada is more uniform across regions and communities — a written grammar therefore serves as a stable, shared standard
- **Two reforms advocated and implemented in this book:**
  1. Reduce the use of Sanskrit loanwords — writers must exercise restraint in borrowing Sanskrit vocabulary unnecessarily; native Dravidian alternatives should be preferred
  2. Respell Sanskrit borrowings that remain: write them as most Kannadigas actually pronounce them, not in Sanskrit orthography. This makes written Kannada more natural and accessible to all
- **New terminology:** Bhat uses entirely native Kannada grammatical terms throughout (e.g. *hesar padu* for noun, *esaka padu* for verb, *ittaLa* for structure/morphology, *ottugaLu* for suffixes). He acknowledges this may briefly disorient readers used to Sanskrit-derived terms like *nāmapada*, *kriyāpada*, *vibhakti* — but argues the native terms are more transparent and should become standard
- **Table of contents (ಒಳಪಿಡಿ) visible in transcript:** 19 chapters covering:
  - Ch. 1: Overview (*Munnōṭa*) — nature of written Kannada, sentence structure
  - Ch. 2: Structure of nouns (*Hesar padagaLa iTTaLa*) — compound words, pairs
  - Ch. 3: Structure of other word classes (*ULida padagaLa iTTaLa*) — verbs, pronouns, demonstratives, numerals, loanwords
  - Ch. 4: Word-forms (*Padagaļa padarūpagaLu*) — case forms, verb endings, participial forms
  - Ch. 5: Use of verbs (*Esaka padagaLa baLake*) — tense, aspect, mood, voice
  - Chs. 6–8: Noun-phrase structure (*Hesar kantegaLa iTTaLa*), Verb-phrase structure (*Esaku padagaLa pāngugaLu*), Modifications to argument structure
  - Chs. 9–10: Pronouns, demonstratives
  - Chs. 11–12: Adjectives, numerals
  - Chs. 13–18: Embedded clauses, sentence coordination, negation, question sentences, conditional sentences, exclamatory sentences
  - Ch. 19: Conclusion (*Muktāya*)

## Part 2 — The Structure of Written Kannada: Letters, Words, and Word-Classes

[Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=h5VFWQQfPO8)
[Transcript →](../kn/full#part-2)

> **Coverage note:** Part 2 is uncleaned auto-captions. Covers Chapter 1 sections 1.2.1 through 1.2.6 — a systematic introduction to how letters form words, words form phrases, and what functions the main word classes serve.

- **Three levels of grammatical structure (§1.2):**
  1. *Bariges* (letters/phonemes) → *padagaLu* (words) → *padakanteagaLu* (word-groups/phrases) → *vākyagaLu* (sentences)
  2. Within words: derivation by suffixes (*ottugaLu*) and compounding (*jōDu padagaLu*)
  3. Words inflect (*padarūpagaLu*) to show their grammatical relationships within sentences
- **The 31-letter inventory of "everyone's Kannada" (*ellara kannaDa*):** Bhat specifies that standard written Kannada uses exactly 31 phonemic letters — 10 vowels (*tereyu/igi*, i.e. open sounds) and 21 consonants (*muccuyuLi*, closed sounds). This 31-letter system deliberately excludes the Sanskrit aspirate consonants (*mahāprāṇa*) and the retroflex sibilant (*ṣa*) that appear in Sanskrit loanwords — a direct implementation of the spelling reform he advocates
- **Words: with and without internal structure (§1.2.2):** Kannada words divide into
  - *iTTaLaviruvā padagaLu* — words with analyzable internal structure (e.g. *cikkamma* = *cikka* + *amma*; *kuṇita* = *kuṇi* + suffix *-ta*)
  - *iTTaLavillada padagaLu* — words with no analyzable internal structure (e.g. *tale*, *manE*)
  - Compositional phrases (*kogetagaLu*, descriptive noun-groups) always have an internal structure; their meaning can be read directly from that structure. But compound words (*jōDu padagaLu*) have non-compositional meanings (*cikkappa* = father's younger brother, not "small father")
- **Three core word-class functions (§1.2.3–1.2.5):**
  1. **Naming** (*hesarisu*): *hesar padagaLu* (nouns) identify persons, things, or concepts. Nouns may be *koTTa hesaru* (proper names: Jānaki, Maisūru, Kāvēri) or *iruvā hesaru* (common nouns: *mara*, *huDuga*, *sīre*)
     - Identification can also be done through a descriptive phrase (*kogetā*): *aLa geLatiy* ("her friend"), *hasuru sīre* ("green sari")
  2. **Predicating** (*esaki telisu*): *esaka/yesaka padagaLu* (verbs) express actions or states. Verbs cannot be identified by name (proper/common) — they express what is happening or being done
  3. **Qualifying** (*paricaya telisu*): *paricaya padagaLu* (adjectives/adverbs) add descriptions to nouns or verbs. Used within descriptive phrases (*hasuru sīre*) and with existential copulas (*Rājuvina kaṇṇu kempu ide* — "Raju's eye is red")
- **Additional word classes (§1.2.6):**
  - *āDu padagaLu* (pronouns for speaker/addressee): *nānu* (I), *nīnu* (you)
  - *tōru padagaLu* (demonstratives): *idu/adu* (this/that), *ivaru/avaru* (these people/those people), *īga/āga* (now/then), *iSTu/aSTu* (this much/that much)
  - *eNike padagaLu* (numerals): share properties of both adjectives and nouns
  - *aNaka padagaLu* (ideophones/sound-symbolic words): *dagadagane uriyu* ("burn blazingly"), *DabbanebiTTu* ("fell with a thud") — primarily used as adverbs but can combine with *-isu* to form verbs (*guNuguTTu*)

## Part 3 — Word-Forms and Sentence Structure

[Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6EBf9mJG4qI)
[Transcript →](../kn/full#part-3)

> **Coverage note:** Part 3 is uncleaned auto-captions. Covers §1.2.7 (inflectional morphology), §1.2.8 (phrase structure), and §1.3 (sentence structure) — the heart of Bhat's syntactic description.

- **Inflectional word-forms (*padarūpagaLu*) (§1.2.7):**
  - Nouns inflect for case: *mara* (nominative), *maravanu* (accusative), *maradinda* (ablative), *marakke* (dative), *maradalli* (locative), *marada* (genitive). These forms signal the noun's grammatical role relative to the verb
  - Verbs inflect far more richly. For *hattu* ("climb"), the forms include: *hatTida* (past, masculine agent), *hatTitu* (past, non-human agent), *hatTuttade* (present/future), *hatTali* (hortative/permissive), *hatTisidaru* (causative past). The agreement suffixes at the end (*-a*, *-itu*, *-aru* etc.) encode the gender/class of the agent; the tense suffixes in the middle (*-id-*, *-utt-*) encode the time relation between utterance and event
  - Causative *-isu* marks that someone caused another to perform the action; hortative *-ali* marks speaker's consent
- **Phrase structure (*padakanteagaLa iTTaLa*) (§1.2.8):**
  - A sentence cannot be properly analyzed at the word level alone — it must be broken into *padakanteagaLu* (constituents/phrases)
  - Example: *cikka huDuga hattu roTTi tinda* — the five words group as [*cikka huDuga*] + [*hattu roTTi*] + *tinda*. The two noun-phrases each identify a participant in the eating event; the verb is the predicate
  - Structural ambiguity arises when the same words can group differently: *huli bāyige bidda Dhanada karu kūgāDuttide* is two-ways ambiguous — "the calf of a cow that fell into the tiger's mouth is crying" vs. "the calf of the cow that fell into the tiger's mouth is crying" (i.e. whether *huli bāyige bidda* modifies *Dhana* or *karu*)
- **Sentence types (*vākyagaLa iTTaLa*) (§1.3):**
  - *suLu vākyagaLu* (simple sentences), *jōDu vākyagaLu* (coordinate complex sentences), *sikkalu vākyagaLu* (embedded/subordinate complex sentences)
  - All other sentence types are derived from simple declarative sentences by modification
  - Sentence modality (from a single declarative base): question (add *-ō*), hortative/permission (add *-ali*), request for permission (add *-alō*), prohibition (negate + *irali*)
- **Argument structure (*pāngugaLu*) (§1.3.2):**
  - Each verb takes a specific set of *pāngugaLu* (arguments/participants). These are:
    - *āgu* (undergoer/subject of intransitive): marked by zero (no suffix needed)
    - *māDu* (agent/subject of transitive): marked by zero
    - *īDu* (object/patient): marked by *-annu* (often deletable, e.g. *Rāju mara kaDida*)
    - *suru* (source): marked by *-inda*
    - *guri* (goal): marked by *-ge*
    - *jāga* (location): marked by *-alli*
  - Verb classes determine which arguments occur: *sōru* (leak) takes only *āgu* + optional *suru*; *ōdu* (read) takes *māDu* + *īDu*; *māru* (sell) takes *māDu*, *guri*, and *īDu*; *aLu* (cry) takes only *āgu*
  - Existential sentences (*iruha vākyagaLu*) have their own argument set: *iruga* (the thing that exists), plus *jāga* (location) or *hotte* (time), or a *paricaya* (quality/property), e.g. *huDuganu sappagiddāne* ("the boy is quiet"), *avanige kelasa ide* ("he has work")
