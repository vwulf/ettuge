# The Dot That Counted: Dravidian Script Architecture and the Origins of Zero

*A speculative research essay linking the puḷḷi (virama dot), śūnya etymology, and the K-127 Cambodia inscription*

---

## Abstract

The conventional narrative attributes the mathematical zero to Sanskrit philosophical vocabulary (*śūnya* — emptiness) as elaborated through Buddhist *śūnyatā* doctrine and formalised arithmetically by Brahmagupta (628 CE). This essay argues that the conceptual and graphic substrate for zero may be substantially older and Dravidian in origin: the *puḷḷi* (Tamil virama dot, attested from the 2nd century CE), the possible Dravidian etymology of *śūnya* itself, and the divergent treatment of positional zero between Tamil and Kannada numeral traditions all point toward a richer, pre-Buddhist infrastructure for the concept of marked absence. The essay further examines the Khmer inscription K-127 (683 CE) — the earliest dated zero in stone — through the lens of the Pallava-Kadamba script controversy, and draws parallels between the adoption of the positional zero and the late appearance of the visible virama in the Kadamba-to-Kannada script transition.

---

## 1. Introduction: The Standard Story and Its Gaps

The story of zero as commonly told runs: emptiness as Buddhist philosophy (*śūnyatā*, c. 3rd century BCE) → placeholder dot in calculation (*śūnya-bindu*, Bakhshali manuscript, 3rd–4th century CE) → arithmetic zero with defined rules (Brahmagupta, *Brahmasphutasiddhānta*, 628 CE) → stone inscription zero (Gwalior temple, 876 CE).

Amir Aczel's *Finding Zero* (2015) added a crucial chapter: the earlier Khmer inscription K-127 (683 CE), bearing a zero in positional notation predating the Gwalior zero by nearly two centuries. Aczel's quest — recovering the stone from Phnom Penh — dramatised the question of where the zero symbol *originated* before reaching Cambodia.

What this narrative largely sidesteps are three questions that the Dravidian linguistic and epigraphic record forces us to confront:

1. Does *śūnya* have a plausible Dravidian etymology that predates its Sanskrit philosophical elaboration?
2. Was the *puḷḷi* (virama dot) — an earlier, independent, visible mark for "absence in a structured positional slot" — a cognitive and graphic precursor to the zero symbol?
3. Does the divergence between Tamil (which retained dedicated non-positional glyphs for 10/100/1000 and never natively adopted positional zero) and Kannada (which adopted positional zero ~9th–10th century CE) reflect a deeper structural difference in how each tradition encoded absence?

## 2. The Etymology of *Śūnya*: Sanskrit or Dravidian?

### 2.1 The standard internal Sanskrit derivation

Sanskrit *śūnya* is conventionally derived from the root *śvi* / *śū-* (to swell, to be hollow), giving *śū-na* (hollow, swollen) → *śūnya* (empty, void). The proposed Proto-Indo-European cognate *\*ḱeu-* (to swell) links to Greek *κύω* and English *swollen* — but this connection is weak and contested in the scholarly literature. Crucially, Mayrhofer's authoritative *Etymologisches Wörterbuch des Altindoarischen* (1992–2001) — the standard reference for Sanskrit etymologies — flags words without clean Indo-European cognates as candidates for Dravidian substrate influence.

*Śūnya* has no clean IE cognate family. No Greek, Latin, Avestan, or Germanic word clearly reflects *\*śū-na* in the sense of "empty/void." This is precisely the criterion Caldwell (1856), Kittel (1873), and Emeneau (DEDR, 1984) use to identify Dravidian loans into Sanskrit.

### 2.2 The Dravidian cluster: *suḻi*, *suḻiyam*, *sonne*

Consider the following cross-Dravidian word family:

| Language | Word | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Tamil | *suḻi* | whirl, curl, vortex, spiral |
| Tamil | *suḻiyam* | zero (the numeral) — coined from *suḻi*, describing the *shape* of ௦ |
| Kannada | *sonne* (ಸೊನ್ನೆ) | zero (numeral only; not used for existential nothingness) |
| Telugu | *sūnnu* | zero |

The Tamil *suḻiyam* is remarkable: it names zero from the *shape* of the symbol (a curl/whirl) rather than from philosophical emptiness. This is a concrete, visual, physically motivated etymology — exactly what you would expect from a root that predates philosophical abstraction. The *suḻi* root (Proto-Dravidian *\*cul-* / *\*suḻ-*, rotating/circular) generates the semantic field: circular → hollow at centre → empty → void.

The Kannada *sonne* is most plausibly a tadbhava of *śūnya* — but *if* śūnya itself is a Sanskritisation of a Dravidian *\*sul-/suḻ-* root, then *sonne* is not a borrowing from Sanskrit at all: it is the original Dravidian root resurfacing in its Kannada phonological form (Dravidian *s-* → Kannada *s-*; *u* → *o* vowel shift; *-ya* → *-ne* suffix adaptation).

The phonological path *suḻi* → *śūnya* would require:
- *s* → *ś* (palatalisation, common in Sanskrit contact)
- *uḻ* → *ū* (loss of retroflex lateral, vowel lengthening)
- *-i* → *-ya* (Sanskrit nominalising suffix applied to borrowed root)

This is entirely within the documented patterns of Dravidian-to-Sanskrit phonological adaptation. The standard criteria for identifying Dravidian loans in Sanskrit — absence of IE cognates, presence of the word across multiple Dravidian branches — are both satisfied by *śūnya*.

### 2.3 Chronological layering

The word *śūnya* appears in Sanskrit in three distinct phases:

1. **Atharvaveda (~1200–900 BCE):** Concrete, mundane usage — *śūnya* as empty/deserted place, abandoned house. No philosophical weight.
2. **Early Upaniṣads (~800–600 BCE):** Philosophical elaboration begins. The *pūrṇam adaḥ pūrṇam idaṃ* verse (Bṛhadāraṇyaka 5.1.1, ~700 BCE) crystallises the *śūnya/pūrṇa* polarity as a cosmological framework — notably using *pūrṇa* (fullness) as the metaphysical positive term, with *śūnya* as its implied complement.
3. **Early Buddhist texts (~5th–3rd century BCE):** *Suññatā* (Pāli) becomes a technical term in the Theravāda *Paṭisambhidāmagga* (~3rd century BCE), treating emptiness as the not-self (*anattā*) nature of the five aggregates. Nāgārjuna's Mādhyamaka (~2nd century CE) then develops *śūnyatā* into a complete philosophical system.

This layering is consistent with a word that entered Sanskrit from a Dravidian substrate at a mundane, concrete level, was then philosophically elaborated, and finally mathematised. The Buddhist *suññatā* would not have *created* the word but inherited and transformed it.

## 3. The *Puḷḷi* Hypothesis: Absence as Scribal Architecture

### 3.1 The Tamil puḷḷi and its uniqueness

The Tamil *puḷḷi* (புள்ளி, virama dot) is attested in inscriptions from the **2nd century CE** (Mahadevan 2003, *Early Tamil Epigraphy*), making it one of the earliest explicit visible absence-markers in any South Asian script. It marks "no vowel follows this consonant" — a visible dot encoding positional absence within a structured system.

What makes the Tamil puḷḷi uniquely significant is that, unlike viramas in other Brahmic scripts (where the absence mark is typically *hidden* inside a conjunct ligature), the Tamil puḷḷi is **always visibly rendered as a dot above the consonant**. As the Unicode mailing list discussion (N. Ganesan, 2011) notes: *"The virama is explicit in Tamil, but all other scripts have mostly hidden virama due to conjunct formation."*

Furthermore, Tamil grammar explicitly theorises the puḷḷi from the earliest period. The *Tolkāppiyam* treats the bare consonant (with puḷḷi) as a first-class grammatical entity — *mey* (dead body/consonant) — philosophically distinguished from *uyir* (soul/vowel). Consonants are "dead bodies" without the "soul" of a vowel: the puḷḷi marks this death. The *Piṅkalantai nikaNTu* (9th century CE) is the oldest Indian text to use *virāma* specifically for the "dead consonant with puḷḷi."

The word *puḷḷi* itself derives from *pul-* (small, insignificant, minimal) — cognate with Kannada *hullu* (grass, trivial, low). The puḷḷi is the *smallest meaningful mark* — the minimal visible unit. This is also what *cukke* means in Kannada: both "dot/point" and "star" (a dot of light in the distance) — the minimum perceptible mark.

### 3.2 The structural isomorphism

The puḷḷi and the zero perform structurally identical operations in their respective systems:

| System | Mark | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Abugida script | puḷḷi / virama | "Absence of vowel in this phonological slot" |
| Positional numeral | ০ / ० / ೦ | "Absence of quantity in this positional slot" |

Both are marks for *absence within a structured positional system*. Both encode "nothing here" as a **first-class visible entity** rather than leaving the slot blank. This is the key cognitive innovation — not the philosophical concept of emptiness (which many cultures had), but the *scribal discipline* of marking absence explicitly as a form of presence.

The Bakhshali manuscript zero (3rd–4th century CE) is a dot — graphically identical to the Tamil puḷḷi. The coincidence of form invites the hypothesis that the scribal tradition of marking positional absence as a dot migrated from phonological notation into numerical notation. The two systems coexisted in the same literate community (Brahmin scholars who both read inscriptions and did mathematics), and the dot was already an established sign for "nothing in this slot."

### 3.3 Why Tamil resisted positional zero

The Tamil numeral system retained dedicated glyphs for 10 (௰), 100 (௱), and 1000 (௲) and never natively adopted positional zero within a traditional framework. Modern Tamil ௦ is essentially a contemporary adoption. This is structurally parallel to Tamil's retention of the visible puḷḷi: both reflect a system that already had a **rich infrastructure for encoding absence explicitly**, and therefore had less need to import a new symbol.

Tamil's additive system (using dedicated signs for powers of ten plus multiplicative combination) was complete and functional. It was not deficient — it was *differently* complete. The positional system's key advantage (compactness, calculational efficiency) did not outweigh the disruption to an existing orthographic tradition that was already marking absence in a neighbouring system.

Kannada, by contrast, handled the virama differently: consonant clusters were encoded through **subjoined ligature stacking** — the virama was *hidden* inside the conjunct form, invisible in the rendered glyph. The explicit halanta (್) became consistently visible in Kannada inscriptions only from the **Rashtrakuta period (8th–10th century CE)** — precisely when positional zero was also being adopted (~9th–10th century CE). The two innovations — explicit absence-marking in script and explicit absence-marking in numerals — arrived together.

## 4. The Cambodia Connection: K-127 and the Pallava-Kadamba Debate

### 4.1 K-127 and its zero

Khmer inscription K-127, dated 683 CE (Śaka year 605), discovered at Sambor on the Mekong River, contains the earliest dated zero in stone:

> *"...the Śaka era has reached 605..."*

The numeral 605 is written with a positional zero in the hundreds place — unambiguous place-value notation with zero as a placeholder. This antedates the Gwalior temple zero (876 CE) by nearly two centuries, and complicates any account that places the *origin* of symbolic zero in northern India.

The inscription is in **early Khmer script**, written in Sanskrit. The script used in 7th-century Cambodia for Sanskrit was directly derived from southern Indian scripts transmitted via trade and religious contact.

### 4.2 The script attribution controversy

The script of K-127 and related Cambodian inscriptions is conventionally attributed to **"Pallava Grantha"** — the prestige Sanskrit-writing script of the Pallava dynasty (Kanchipuram, 4th–9th century CE). However, several scholars have raised serious objections to this attribution:

**The Kadamba case:**

Epigrapher Arlo Griffiths (referenced in Pallava script Wikipedia article) argues that the "Pallava" label is **misleading**, preferring the term *Late Southern Brāhmī* for the cluster of related scripts. The skyknowledge.com paleographic analysis notes explicitly: *"although the emerging script has also been called proto-Telugu, proto-Kannada, Kadamba, a form of Grantha, or simply Southern Brahmi, it seems certain that during the reign of the Pallava kings, the script in question accompanied priests, monks, scholars and traders into SE Asia."*

The *Controversies in History* blog analysis of Pallava epigraphy makes the sharpest case:

- The earliest Pallava inscriptions (Mayidavolu copper plates, 4th century CE) use **Kadamba script**, not a distinct "Pallava" script
- *"Grantha and Vatteluthu are variations of Kadamba script"* — they evolved *from* Kadamba, not independently
- The "box-headed alphabet" found in inscriptions of King Bhadravarman of Champa (central Vietnam) is *"not characteristic of Pallava inscriptions, but is generally found in central and upper Deccan as well as Kannada-speaking areas"*
- The Śaka era used in Cambodian and Indonesian inscriptions was **introduced by the Chalukyas of Badami** (Karnataka) — it was never used by the Pallavas
- The *-varman* suffix in Southeast Asian dynastic names is not exclusive to Pallavas but is shared by Kadambas, Gangas, Chalukyas, and many other Deccan dynasties

**The Grantha-ottakṣara connection:**

Grantha script (used for Sanskrit in Tamil Nadu) developed the *ottakṣara* — stacked subjoined consonant forms for representing Sanskrit consonant clusters — which are structurally closer to the conjunct-stacking tradition of Kadamba/Kannada than to the older Tamil-Brāhmī orthography. The Khmer script's handling of consonant clusters through subjoined forms (visible in any Khmer font today) is architecturally closer to this Kadamba-Grantha tradition of conjunct stacking than to the Tamil puḷḷi approach of leaving the virama explicit.

**The political connection:**

Crucially, there was a **direct dynastic connection** between the Pallavas and Cambodia. After Pallava king Parameśvaravarman II died without heir (~730 CE), representatives travelled to Kambujadeśa, then ruled by **King Kadaveśa Harivarma** — who was of Pallava lineage, but the very name *Kadaveśa* (lord of the Kadavas) points to the Kadava/Kadamba connection. *Pallavamalla* (Nandivarman II) was the prince who returned from Cambodia to claim the Pallava throne. The Pallava-Cambodia-Kadamba triangle is genealogical, not merely scribal.

**The Tamil kings but not Tamil script:**

Cambodia was indeed substantially influenced by Tamil-speaking merchants, priests, and kings — the "Tamil kings" argument is well-attested in scholarship (Himansu Bhanu Sarkar, 1969; Jan Wisseman Christie, 1998). But the script transmitted was **not the Tamil script** (which had its own phonological constraints and lacked aspirated consonants). What was transmitted was the **Grantha/Kadamba tradition** for writing Sanskrit — the prestige religious and administrative language. Tamil kings carried a *Sanskrit-writing system*, not their vernacular script.

This matters for the zero question: the script of K-127 that **contains the zero** is a Sanskrit-writing script in the Kadamba-Grantha tradition — architecturally structured with subjoined consonant clusters and a treatment of virama closer to the Kadamba/Kannada implicit-virama approach than to the Tamil explicit-puḷḷi approach.

## 5. Synthesis: A Dravidian Archaeology of Zero

Assembling the evidence, we can sketch a speculative but coherent genealogy:

### 5.1 The conceptual substrate (~1200 BCE – 500 BCE)

*Śūnya* enters Sanskrit from a Dravidian *\*suḻ-/sul-* root (circular, hollow, spiral) at the mundane level in the Atharvaveda period. The word carries the concrete sense of a hollow space — a bowl, an empty vessel, a vortex with nothing at its centre. It does **not** yet carry philosophical weight.

Simultaneously, the *pūrṇa/śūnya* polarity crystallises in the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (~700 BCE): "the full" and "the hollow" as metaphysical complements. The mathematical content of the *pūrṇam adaḥ* verse (∞ − ∞ = ∞) implies an implicit concept of zero as identity element, though not yet symbolised.

### 5.2 The scribal infrastructure (~300 BCE – 200 CE)

Tamil-Brāhmī adapts the Brāhmī script for Dravidian phonology and makes a distinctive innovation: the **visible puḷḷi**, a dot that marks "no vowel here." This is attested by the 2nd century CE (Mahadevan 2003) and theorised explicitly in *Tolkāppiyam* grammar as the *mey* (dead consonant) — a first-class entity, not an absence but a marked presence of nothing.

The puḷḷi is graphically a **dot** — the same form the Bakhshali manuscript (3rd–4th century CE) uses for zero.

### 5.3 The mathematical transition (~300 CE – 630 CE)

The dot migrates from phonological notation to numerical notation. It appears in the Bakhshali manuscript as *śūnya-sthāna* ("empty place"), functioning as a placeholder. Brahmagupta (628 CE) formalises arithmetic rules for zero but does not use a symbol in his text — he uses the word *śūnya*.

The philosophical preparation (Buddhist *śūnyatā*, Upaniṣadic *pūrṇa/śūnya* polarity) provided the **conceptual permission** to treat nothingness as a valid mathematical entity. The puḷḷi provided the **graphic precedent** for marking positional absence as a visible symbol.

### 5.4 The Cambodia inscription (~683 CE)

K-127 uses positional zero in the Kadamba-Grantha scribal tradition. The script carries the implicit-virama architecture of Kadamba (consonant clusters handled through subjoined forms), and the zero is used confidently in a Sanskrit dateline — suggesting it was already an established convention, not a novelty.

The transmission route was via the **Pallava-Kadamba-Chalukya scribal complex** of the South Indian Deccan, carried to Southeast Asia by Brahmin priests, merchants, and itinerant scholars. The Śaka era (a Chalukyan invention) used in K-127's dateline confirms this Karnataka-Deccan connection over a purely Tamil-Nadu/Pallava route.

### 5.5 The Tamil/Kannada numeral divergence (~9th–10th century CE)

Tamil retains its additive numeral system with dedicated glyphs for 10/100/1000 and does not adopt positional zero natively. This is structurally parallel to Tamil's retention of the explicit visible puḷḷi: a tradition that already marks absence explicitly doesn't need a second system for the same purpose.

Kannada adopts positional zero (~9th–10th century CE), contemporaneously with the explicit halanta becoming consistently visible in inscriptions. Both are **late explicit-absence-marking innovations** — Kannada arriving at what Tamil had always had (a visible absence marker), but through a different route: importing it from the mathematical tradition rather than developing it from the scribal one.

## 6. The *Pūrṇam Adaḥ* Paradox

The Bṛhadāraṇyaka verse (*pūrṇam adaḥ pūrṇam idaṃ...*, ~700 BCE) performs a remarkable mathematical operation: it asserts that taking the full from the full leaves the full unchanged. This is structurally identical to the statement that ∞ − ∞ = ∞ — the infinite as an identity element under subtraction of itself.

What the verse does not say is: "the empty from the full leaves the full." But it implies it: if *pūrṇa* minus *pūrṇa* equals *pūrṇa*, then zero is the only number consistent with this arithmetic. The *pūrṇa/śūnya* polarity was the conceptual container that eventually had to be formalised arithmetically — and when it was, it produced the Hindu-Arabic numeral system that the world uses today.

The *śūnya* that appears in this conceptual pair may not be a pure Sanskrit invention. If the root is Dravidian — *\*suḻ-*, the hollow at the centre of the vortex — then the mathematical zero is not only Indian in origin but specifically Dravidian in its conceptual seed, philosophically watered by Buddhism and Sanskrit metaphysics, and graphically grounded in the oldest visible absence-marker in the Indic scribal tradition: the Tamil puḷḷi dot.

## 7. Open Questions and Further Research

This essay has been speculative throughout. The following questions require further investigation:

1. **DEDR entry for *pul-/puḷ-*:** Does Burrow & Emeneau's *Dravidian Etymological Dictionary* contain entries that link *puḷḷi* (dot), *puḷi* (tiger, spotted), and *pul* (insignificant/small) to a reconstructed Proto-Dravidian root? The semantic coherence (minimal mark = spot on an animal = smallest unit) is suggestive but needs philological confirmation.

2. **Śūnya in Mayrhofer:** Does Mayrhofer's *EWAia* entry for *śūnya* flag the absence of IE cognates and mention any Dravidian parallel? If so, this would be the authoritative acknowledgement the hypothesis needs.

3. **K-127 script paleography:** A detailed comparative paleographic analysis of K-127's numeral glyphs — specifically the zero — against contemporary Kadamba, Grantha, and Tamil-Brāhmī numeral forms would clarify the script attribution question. The Grokipedia analysis of Kannada numerals notes that the Gudnapur inscription (Kadamba, ~500 CE) has the earliest complete Kannada numeral set; the visual relationship between Gudnapur numerals and K-127 numerals deserves examination.

4. **Virama appearance timeline in Kadamba:** A systematic survey of Kadamba copper plate inscriptions for the first consistent appearances of explicit virama marks (versus implicit conjunct handling) would test the hypothesis that explicit absence-marking and zero adoption are correlated.

5. **Tamil astronomical texts:** Did Tamil astronomers (working with the *Āryabhaṭīya* tradition after ~499 CE) use the puḷḷi in numerical contexts, or did they switch to a separate zero symbol? The answer would clarify whether the puḷḷi was ever consciously *identified* with zero in Tamil scribal practice.

## Notes on Terminology

**Eke romanisation** (used throughout ettuge): capital letters mark long vowels (A=ā, I=ī, U=ū, E=ē, O=ō); uppercase retroflex consonants (T, D, N, L); S = palatal sibilant.

**Tadbhava:** A Sanskrit-origin word that has been phonologically adapted into a Dravidian language (as opposed to *tatsama*, used unchanged). The hypothesis that *śūnya* is a Sanskritisation of a Dravidian root inverts the usual assumption: *sonne* (Kannada) and *suḻiyam* (Tamil) would not be tadbhavas of Sanskrit *śūnya* but rather cognates of a common Pre-Sanskrit root.

**Ottakṣara:** Grantha term for subjoined consonant forms (conjunct clusters). Structurally similar to Kadamba's consonant-stacking approach. Architecturally distinct from Tamil's explicit puḷḷi method.
